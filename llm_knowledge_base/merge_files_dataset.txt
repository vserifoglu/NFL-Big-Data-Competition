## Description

**Note**: This Big Data Bowl 2026 has two competitions. This competition is the **Analytics competition**. Learn more about the Prediction competition [here](link).

The downfield pass is the crown jewel of American sports. When the ball is in the air, anything can happen, like a touchdown, an interception, or a contested catch. The uncertainty and the importance of the outcome of these plays is what helps keep audiences on the edge of its seat.

The 2026 Big Data Bowl is designed to help the National Football League better understand player movement during the pass play, starting with **when the ball is thrown** and ending when the ball is either caught or ruled incomplete. 

For the offensive team, this means focusing on the targeted receiver, whose job is to move towards the ball landing location in order to complete a catch. For the defensive team, who could have several players moving towards the ball, their jobs are to both prevent the offensive player from making a catch, while also going for the ball themselves. This year's Big Data Bowl asks our fans to help track the movement of these players.

In the **Analytics Competition** of the Big Data Bowl, participants are tasked with describing player movement with the ball in the air in a way that is accessible to coaches and football fans. This could be making a new metric or in the comparison of team or player strategy. As is often the case in Big Data Bowl analytics competitions, participants are encouraged to focus on **one small aspect of the play**, as opposed to trying to capture every aspect of every play. This could include:

- The change of direction of a defensive player
- The acceleration of the receiver
- The way that defensive schemes free players up to make plays

# üèà Big Data Bowl 2026: Dataset Inspection & Engineering Handbook

**Project:** The Void Engine (Zone Coverage Auditing)
**Domain:** NFL Player Tracking Data (Post-Snap/Post-Throw)
**Status:** VALIDATED (with documented constraints)

---

## 1. File Topology & Data Flow

The dataset is split into **Tracking Data** (Time-series) and **Context Data** (Play-level metadata).

### Directory Structure
* **`data/train/`**: Contains the raw tracking data.
    * `input_w[01-09].csv`: **Pre-throw** tracking data (frames leading up to the pass).
    * `output_w[01-09].csv`: **Post-throw** tracking data (frames where the ball is in the air).
    * *Note: Files are paired by Week. Week 1 Input matches Week 1 Output.*
* **`data/supplementary_data.csv`**: The "Rosetta Stone." Contains play-level labels (Coverages, EPA, Penalties).

### The "Stitch" & Time Sparsity
* **Temporal Ratio:** `Input` files are ~2.9x longer than `Output` files.
* **Frame Zeroing:** `input_*.csv` frames end at $N$. The first frame of `output_*.csv` is $1$. The pipeline offsets the output frames by $N$ to create a continuous timeline $1 \to N \to End$.

---

## 2. The "Ghost" Constraint (Attrition Analysis)

**Critical Finding:** The dataset is **Lossy**. Players present in the `Input` file (pre-throw) frequently disappear in the `Output` file (post-throw).

### The Survival Rules (Empirically Proven)
1.  **Targeted Receivers:** 100% Survival Rate. They never disappear.
2.  **Proximity Filter:** The dataset aggressively drops players far from the ball.
    * *Survivors* (Kept in data): Avg distance to landing spot = **8.4 yards**.
    * *Ghosts* (Dropped from data): Avg distance to landing spot = **19.8 yards**.
3.  **The "Danger Zone" (8-15 yards):**
    * There is a transition zone (8-15 yards from catch) where players *may* disappear.
    * *Implication:* If a defender is ~12 yards away from the catch, they might not be graded by the Void Engine because they do not exist in the dataframe.

**Engineering Decision:** The Void Engine treats "Ghosts" as **Exempt**. We assume if the NFL tracking system deemed them irrelevant enough to drop, they were not the primary cause of the defensive breakdown.

---

## 3. Coordinate System & Physics

**Warning:** Raw NFL data is non-standardized regarding direction. This pipeline enforces a **Left-to-Right** normalization.

### Normalization Logic
If `play_direction == 'left'`, the engine flips the geometry so the offense always drives from Left ($X=0$) to Right ($X=120$).

* **X-Axis:** $X_{new} = 120 - X_{raw}$
* **Y-Axis:** $Y_{new} = 53.3 - Y_{raw}$
* **Orientation/Dir:** $\theta_{new} = (\theta_{raw} + 180) \mod 360$

### Derived Geometry
* **Line of Scrimmage (LOS):** Calculated as `ball_land_x - pass_length`.
* **Depth:** $X_{player} - X_{LOS}$.
* **Compression Factor:** Used for "Red Zone Squash."
    $$\text{Compression} = \text{clip}\left(\frac{110 - \text{LOS}}{20}, 0.5, 1.0\right)$$

# Zone Coverage Auditing: The "Reaction Void Engine"

## 1. Problem & Premise

Traditional defensive metrics often evaluate "closing speed" in a vacuum. A defender running 20 mph is considered elite, but raw speed fails to capture the context of the movement relative to the defensive scheme.

**The Problem:** Did the defender sprint because they were out of position (recovering from a mistake)? Or did they sprint to tighten an already perfect coverage window? Without context, "fast" looks the same as "panic."

**The Premise:** A "Void" is not just generic empty space; it is an estimated offensive spatial advantage in the target zone (around the ball's landing point) established at the moment of release. The true measure of a defender's performance is evaluated relative to that void: do they measurably reduce the expected separation before the ball arrives?

**The Goal:** To differentiate between defenders who merely run fast ("Athletes") and defenders who systematically close structural gaps ("Erasers").

In this work, a **Void Score** quantifies how favorable the space is for the offense at the throw, and an **Eraser Score** measures how much an individual defender reduces that score while the ball is in the air. This framework directly targets the competition's focus on understanding defender movement while the ball is in the air, using pre-throw context only as conditioning.


## 2. Methodology & Core Metrics

This project utilizes the Big Data Bowl 2026 tracking data, specifically handling the separation between **Input (Pre-Throw, full physics)** and **Output (Post-Throw, ball-in-air)** files. We employ a **"Context vs. Action"** framework:

### Phase A: The Context (The Void)

Using the final frame of the Input file (The Throw), we calculate the **Void Context Score (S_throw)**.

**Definition:** The Euclidean distance between the targeted receiver and the nearest defender at the exact moment of the quarterback's release.

**Classification:**
- **High Void (>5 yds):** Large pre-throw separation; indicates favorable offensive spacing or an early execution gap.
- **Tight Window (<2 yds):** Minimal pre-throw separation; defense maintains strong leverage at the start of the throw.

### Phase B: The Action (The Movement)

Using the Output file (Ball-in-Air), we track the movement of the relevant defenders relative to the receiver and the landing spot.

**Measurement:** We calculate **S_arrival**, the separation distance between the targeted receiver and the nearest defender at the moment of ball arrival (or the final frame of the play).

## Core Metrics

#### 1. Void Improvement Score (VIS)

Measures the net reduction of the offensive spatial advantage during the ball's flight.
VIS = S_throw ‚àí S_arrival


- **Positive (+5.0):** The defender "healed" the coverage, closing a 5-yard gap to 0 yards.
- **Negative (‚àí2.0):** The defender lost ground, allowing the separation to grow.

#### 2. Closing Efficiency Over Expectation (CEOE)

A role-aware metric that evaluates the rate of closure relative to peer performance.

**Definition:** The change in separation per second of flight time, compared to the league average for defenders with the same **Role** (e.g., Linebacker vs. Cornerback) and **Coverage Type** (Man vs. Zone) starting with a similar Void Context.

**Why it matters:** It normalizes for hangtime. Closing 5 yards on a "rainbow" deep ball is easier than closing 5 yards on a quick slant; CEOE accounts for this by measuring efficiency against the clock.

## The Void Engine: Quantifying Defensive Recovery and Damage Control After the Throw

### 1. Abstract

In modern football analysis, defensive backs are often unfairly penalized for the geometry of the play. A cornerback who allows a catch while playing tight press coverage is graded similarly to a safety who allows a catch after sprinting 20 yards to save a broken play. Current tracking metrics measure proximity at the moment of arrival, but they fail to account for the athleticism required to create that proximity.

This project introduces The Void Engine, a physics-based analytics framework that isolates defensive performance during the "Ball-in-Air" phase. By separating the Context (the separation distance at the moment of the throw) from the Action (the distance erased during flight), we create a new metric: Void Improvement Score (VIS).

Our analysis of the 2026 Big Data Bowl data reveals that VIS is a powerful proxy for "Damage Control." While high closing speed does not always guarantee an incompletion, it drastically reduces offensive efficiency. Specifically, defenders with elite VIS scores save an average of 3.34 yards of YAC (Yards After Catch) on completed passes in medium-void situations compared to low-effort counterparts. This framework allows coaches to identify "Erasers"‚Äîdefenders with the elite recovery speed necessary to clean up schematic breakdowns.

### 2. The Football Problem: "The Unfair Grade"

Imagine two defensive plays that end with a receiver catching the ball with 1 yard of separation:

- **Defender A (The Blanket):** Starts 1 yard away at the snap. Trails the receiver perfectly. Ends 1 yard away.
- **Defender B (The Eraser):** Is schemed 15 yards deep (Cover 3 Safety). Reads the throw late. Sprints 14 yards while the ball is in the air. Ends 1 yard away, making a contested tackle.

**The Data Failure**

Standard metrics view these two plays as identical: Distance at Catch = 1.0 yards.

- Defender A is correctly credited for good coverage.
- Defender B receives no credit for the extraordinary athletic effort required to close a 14-yard gap. In fact, traditional stats might penalize him for allowing the catch, ignoring that he turned a potential touchdown into a 15-yard gain.

**The Solution: Separating Scheme from Execution**

To evaluate defenders fairly, we must decouple the situation they inherited from what they did with it.

- **The Context (Sthrow‚Äã):** How big was the "Void" (separation) at the precise moment the Quarterback released the ball? This is often dictated by the defensive call (Scheme) or a pre-snap mistake.
- **The Recovery (VIS):** How many yards of that Void did the defender "erase" before the ball arrived? This is dictated by reaction time, acceleration, and effort (Player Skill).

By focusing on the change in separation rather than the absolute distance, The Void Engine identifies a specific, undervalued archetype of player: the Eraser, who specializes in mitigating disaster when the primary coverage breaks down.


### 3. Methodology: Building The Void Engine

To quantify recovery speed, we constructed a three-phase pipeline that isolates the "Ball-in-Air" window‚Äîstarting strictly at the `pass_forward` event and ending at `pass_arrived` (catch, incompletion, or interception).

#### Phase A: Context (Sthrow)

Before evaluating a defender‚Äôs movement, we must understand the difficulty of their assignment. We define the "Context" based on the geometry of the field at the precise moment the ball is thrown.

*   **Metric:** Sthrow (Separation at Throw).
*   **Logic:** We calculate the Euclidean distance between the targeted receiver and the single nearest defender.
*   **Design Decision:** We utilize a **Primary Matchup Reduction** strategy. While zone defenses involve multiple players, the responsibility to "squeeze" the catch point falls primarily on the nearest defender. By isolating this specific relationship, we avoid crediting a deep safety for a tight window created by a cornerback, ensuring we grade individual responsibility rather than schematic positioning.

#### Phase B: Action (VIS)

Once the ball is in the air, the "Action" phase begins. We measure how the defender changes their leverage relative to the receiver over the duration of the flight.

*   **Metric:** Void Improvement Score (VIS).
*   **Formula:** `VIS = Sthrow - Sarrival`
*   **Interpretation:**
    *   **Positive VIS (+):** The defender closed space (Erasure).
    *   **Negative VIS (-):** The defender lost leverage (Liability).
    *   **Zero VIS:** The defender maintained their position (Lockdown/Trail).

Unlike max speed, which can be misleading (a defender sprinting because they are beaten), VIS measures *productive velocity*‚Äîspeed that actually reduces the void.

![Race Charts](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/vserifoglu/NFL-Big-Data-Compeition/refs/heads/main/static/visuals_final/V2_Race_Charts.png)

*   **Figure 2:** The Physics of Erasure. This chart illustrates the difference between an Eraser (Green) who closes a 15-yard gap, and a Liability (Red) who allows a neutral start to become a wide-open throw.

#### Phase C: Benchmarking (CEOE)

Raw VIS favors positions that play deep (Safeties) because they have more "room" to erase. To create a fair comparison, we normalize performance against positional expectations.

*   **Metric:** Closing Efficiency Over Expectation (CEOE).
*   **Normalization:** We compare each player‚Äôs average VIS strictly against their positional peers (Cornerbacks vs. Cornerbacks, Linebackers vs. Linebackers).
*   **Bayesian Shrinkage:** To solve the "Small Sample Size" problem, we apply Bayesian Shrinkage to the leaderboard. A player‚Äôs raw score is pulled toward the positional mean based on their snap count (n) and a prior weight (m=20).

    `CEOE = (n * RawScore + m * PositionalAvg) / (n + m)`

This ensures our "Top Erasers" are proven performers with consistent impact, rather than backups with a single lucky play.


### 4. Validation: From "Ball Hawk" to "Damage Control"

A common pitfall in tracking metrics is assuming that closing speed always leads to positive outcomes. Our validation process revealed a crucial nuance in how recovery speed interacts with play results.

#### The "Tackle Bias" Discovery

When we aligned our VIS metric against binary pass outcomes (Complete vs. Incomplete), we observed a counter-intuitive trend in high-void situations:

*   **Completed Passes:** Higher average VIS.
*   **Incomplete Passes:** Lower average VIS.

This phenomenon, which we term **"Tackle Bias,"** occurs because defenders often generate their maximum closing speed specifically when a catch is made, converging rapidly to secure the tackle. Conversely, on uncatchable overthrows, a defender might slow down, registering a lower VIS despite the positive defensive outcome.

#### The Pivot: VIS as a Damage Control Metric

This finding clarified that VIS is not necessarily a predictor of prevention, but of mitigation. To test this, we analyzed the relationship between VIS and Yards After Catch (YAC) on completed passes.

We segmented plays by their initial difficulty (Start Distance) and compared the outcomes of defenders with **High VIS** vs. **Negative VIS**.

**Table 1: YAC Savings by Eraser Tier**

| Context (Start Distance) | Low Effort (Negative VIS) | High Effort (VIS > 3) | YAC Saved |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Medium Void (3-6 yds) | 4.77 yds allowed | 1.43 yds allowed | **+3.34 yds** |
| High Void (6-10 yds) | 4.65 yds allowed | 2.50 yds allowed | **+2.15 yds** |

#### The Verdict

The results confirm that while high recovery speed does not guarantee an incompletion, it drastically reduces the damage of a completion. In the critical "Medium Void" window‚Äîwhere many first downs are decided‚Äîdefenders with elite recovery speed hold receivers to **3.34 fewer yards after the catch** than their slower-reacting peers.

This validates The Void Engine as a measure of defensive insurance: identifying the players who turn potential touchdowns into manageable gains.

### 7. Strategic Applications

The Void Engine provides actionable intelligence for three key pillars of an NFL organization:

#### For Coaches: Trusting the "Island"

Defensive Coordinators often face a dilemma: Can I trust this DB in single-high coverage? CEOE provides the answer.

*   A player with **high Lockdown scores (Low Start, Low End)** can be trusted in Press-Man.
*   A player with **high Eraser scores (High Start, Tight Finish)** is the ideal candidate for Single-High Safety, where covering ground is the primary job description.

#### For Scouting: Finding the Hidden Gem

Draft prospects often fall due to "bad technique" or "poor positioning." However, positioning is coachable; elite recovery speed is not.

*   **The Opportunity:** A prospect with a low PFF grade (due to allowing separation early) but a high CEOE represents a high-value asset. They possess the raw "Eraser" trait that can save a broken play, making them a "High Ceiling" draft pick.

#### For Scheme Design: Heatmap Vulnerabilities

By mapping Average VIS across field zones (Figure 3), coordinators can identify structural weaknesses.

*   **Example:** If the defense has a negative Average VIS on deep outs in Cover 3, it indicates that the outside corners are not recovering fast enough to the sideline. The coordinator can adjust by playing softer (increasing cushion) or rolling coverage.

### 8. Conclusion & Future Work

#### The Verdict

The Void Engine challenges the binary view of defensive coverage. It moves beyond "Open vs. Covered" to quantify the work done to change that status. By isolating the "Ball-in-Air" phase, we successfully turned recovery speed‚Äîpreviously an intangible "eye test" trait‚Äîinto a rigorous, quantifiable asset. We proved that while high recovery speed does not guarantee interceptions, it is a premier tool for **Damage Control**, saving valuable yardage (YAC) when the scheme breaks down.

#### Limitations

Honest data science requires acknowledging constraints:

*   **Blocker Blindness:** Our geometric distance calculation (Sarrival) does not currently account for blockers who might be obstructing the defender‚Äôs path.
*   **Sideline Constraints:** A defender might slow down not because of a lack of effort, but because the receiver is running out of bounds. The current model interprets this deceleration as a loss of leverage.
*   **Quarterback Intent:** We assume the QB aims for the receiver. On back-shoulder throws, "separation" is actually desirable for the offense, which creates noise in the "Tight Window" metric.

#### Future Work

The next iteration of The Void Engine would integrate **Orientation (o)** and **Direction (dir)** tracking data to create a "**Look-Off Penalty**." By measuring if a defender‚Äôs hips were turned the wrong way at the throw, we could further isolate the difficulty of the recovery, rewarding players who erase gaps even after biting on a play-action fake.
